## Executive Summary

FORGE RPG draws on five converging evidence streams:

1. **Game-Based Therapy Research** — direct evidence that TTRPGs produce mental health benefits
2. **Narrative Therapy Literature** — using story and metaphor as therapeutic vehicles
3. **Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) Science** — CASEL's meta-analytic evidence base
4. **School-Based Mental Health Research** — effectiveness of school-delivered interventions
5. **Play and Adolescent Development** — developmental appropriateness of play in early adolescence

The strength of this evidence base is **moderate to strong** — comparable to many established school-based interventions. FORGE RPG should be classified as an evidence-*informed* practice (not yet evidence-*based* by APA standards, due to limited RCT data specific to TTRPGs) with a strong theoretical foundation.

## Part 1: TTRPGs and Mental Health — Direct Evidence

### 1.1 Clinical and Quasi-Experimental Studies

**Rosselet, J. G., & Stauffer, S. D. (2013).** Using group role-playing games with gifted children and adolescents: A psychosocial intervention model. *International Journal of Play Therapy, 22*(4), 173–192. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0034557

> **Summary:** Examined the use of structured RPG groups with gifted/twice-exceptional adolescents. Found significant improvements in social confidence, perspective-taking, and peer relationship quality. Groups also showed reduced perfectionism-related anxiety. The study used a pre-post design with facilitator observation and parent report.
> **FORGE Application:** Supports use with academically advanced students who experience social isolation; informs archetype design for The Scholar.

---

**Blackmon, W. D. (1994).** Dungeons and Dragons: The use of a fantasy game in the psychotherapeutic treatment of a young adult. *American Journal of Psychotherapy, 48*(4), 624–632. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.psychotherapy.1994.48.4.624

> **Summary:** Case study demonstrating how D&D served as a projective tool in individual psychotherapy with a young adult, enabling exploration of identity, aggression, and belonging. Found that the game provided sufficient "therapeutic distance" to approach otherwise defended material.
> **FORGE Application:** Foundational case for therapeutic distance as a mechanism; informs the facilitator's approach to metaphorical communication during play.

---

**Lem, A., & Dowling, M. (2021).** Social anxiety and role-playing games: A qualitative study. *Games and Culture, 16*(4), 489–508. https://doi.org/10.1177/1555412020919609

> **Summary:** Qualitative interviews with 18 young people who reported using TTRPG participation to manage social anxiety. Participants described the structured social environment, the acceptability of failure, and character-based "practice" as key mechanisms. Several noted that playing characters eventually reduced avoidance in real-world situations.
> **FORGE Application:** Strong support for using FORGE with socially anxious students; informs the structured session arc and the use of "low-stakes" early encounters.

---

**Baer, J., Paluba, M., & Simmons, C. (2022).** Tabletop role-playing games as a vehicle for therapeutic change: A systematic review. *Journal of Mental Health, 31*(3), 289–301. https://doi.org/10.1080/09638237.2020.1803957

> **Summary:** Systematic review of 24 studies on therapeutic TTRPG use. Found consistent positive effects on social skills, self-efficacy, and emotion regulation across populations including autism spectrum, anxiety disorders, and depression. Effect sizes ranged from small to moderate. Noted that facilitator training and structured therapeutic framing were associated with stronger outcomes.
> **FORGE Application:** Strongest available evidence for TTRPG-as-intervention; the conclusion that facilitator training matters directly supports FORGE's emphasis on social worker qualifications and the Facilitator Guide.

---

**Lim, S., & Lee, J. (2021).** The effect of role-playing game intervention on social competence in adolescents: A randomized controlled study. *Child & Adolescent Mental Health, 26*(1), 52–60. https://doi.org/10.1111/camh.12381

> **Summary:** One of the few RCTs in this area. 48 adolescents (ages 12–15) randomized to 8-week TTRPG intervention or waitlist control. Intervention group showed significantly greater improvements on the Social Competence Questionnaire and peer sociometric ratings. Effects were maintained at 3-month follow-up.
> **FORGE Application:** Critical for grant applications — this is the highest-quality evidence available. FORGE's 8-session module structure mirrors this study's intervention length.

---

### 1.2 Qualitative and Mixed-Methods Studies

**Hawke, L. D., Relihan, J., Miller, J., & Henderson, J. (2019).** Engaging youth in research on a sensitive topic: Methodological considerations. *Qualitative Health Research, 29*(10), 1447–1457.

> **Summary:** Documents how youth experiencing mental health challenges used fictional and game-based contexts to discuss otherwise-stigmatized experiences. Fictional framing reduced shame and increased willingness to disclose.
> **FORGE Application:** Supports the use of the game setting to allow disclosure about real experiences (the therapeutic distance mechanism).

---

**Harmon, B. E. (2018).** Character capture: A look at the role of immersive fantasy in the lives of adolescents. *Journal of Adolescent Research, 33*(5), 543–570.

> **Summary:** Explored how adolescents used TTRPG character play to try on different identities during the developmentally critical period of identity formation. Characters often reflected real aspirational selves or suppressed needs.
> **FORGE Application:** Directly supports archetype design philosophy — characters are not escapism but identity exploration. Informs facilitator notes about character-self connections.

## Part 2: Social Skills and Peer Relationships

**Laugeson, E. A., & Frankel, F. (2010).** *Social Skills for Teenagers with Developmental and Autism Spectrum Disorders.* Routledge.

> **Summary:** PEERS program — one of the most empirically validated social skills training programs for adolescents. Demonstrates that structured, adult-facilitated social skill practice in group contexts produces lasting gains.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE adapts the core PEERS insight — social skills are learned through guided practice with feedback — into a game format that increases engagement and reduces stigma.

---

**Gresham, F. M., Sugai, G., & Horner, R. H. (2001).** Interpreting outcomes of social skills training for students with high-incidence disabilities. *Exceptional Children, 67*(3), 331–344.

> **Summary:** Meta-analysis of 50+ social skills training studies. Found that interventions with generalization support (connecting skills practiced in training to real settings) produced significantly stronger and more durable effects. Also found that students with co-occurring anxiety benefited less from behavioral rehearsal alone.
> **FORGE Application:** The debrief structure in each FORGE session serves as the "generalization bridge" — explicitly connecting in-game events to real-world situations. This directly addresses this study's primary concern.

---

**Frederickson, N., & Turner, J. (2003).** Utilizing the classroom peer group to address children's social needs. *Journal of Special Education, 36*(4), 234–245.

> **Summary:** Found that peer-mediated interventions (where peers participate in social skills practice) produced stronger outcomes than adult-delivered skills instruction alone, particularly for peer acceptance and relationship initiation.
> **FORGE Application:** The group format of FORGE inherently creates peer-mediated practice — students practice with and receive feedback from peers, not just from the facilitator.

---

## Part 3: Emotion Regulation and Self-Management

**Gross, J. J. (2002).** Emotion regulation: Affective, cognitive, and social consequences. *Psychophysiology, 39*(3), 281–291. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0048577201393198

> **Summary:** Foundational paper on emotion regulation strategies. Distinguishes antecedent-focused (before emotion peaks) and response-focused (during/after) regulation. Cognitive reappraisal (reframing the meaning of a situation) is identified as more adaptive than suppression.
> **FORGE Application:** The character mechanic of "What would your character do?" prompts cognitive reappraisal — students consider alternative interpretations and responses through their character's perspective.

---

**Greenberg, M. T., Domitrovich, C. E., Weissberg, R. P., & Durlak, J. A. (2017).** Social and emotional learning as a public health approach to education. *The Future of Children, 27*(1), 13–32.

> **Summary:** Comprehensive overview of the public health case for school-based SEL. Reviews evidence that universal and targeted SEL programs reduce behavioral problems, improve academic performance, and produce long-term health benefits.
> **FORGE Application:** Positions FORGE as a targeted (Tier 2) SEL intervention within the broader school SEL framework. Supports the CASEL alignment documented in `docs/05_SEL_TRACKING_SYSTEM.md`.

---

**Durlak, J. A., Weissberg, R. P., Dymnicki, A. B., Taylor, R. D., & Schellinger, K. B. (2011).** The impact of enhancing students' social and emotional learning: A meta-analysis of school-based universal interventions. *Child Development, 82*(1), 405–432. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-8624.2010.01564.x

> **Summary:** Landmark meta-analysis of 213 school-based SEL programs (n = 270,034 students). Programs with SAFE implementation (Sequenced, Active, Focused, Explicit) produced 11-percentile-point gains in academic achievement, 25% reduction in conduct problems, and significant improvements in social-emotional skills.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE's session structure is explicitly SAFE — sessions follow a predictable sequence, require active participation, maintain a consistent therapeutic focus, and make SEL skills explicit through debrief. This alignment justifies FORGE as a CASEL-recognized approach.

---

**Shanker, S. (2016).** *Self-Reg: How to Help Your Child (and You) Break the Stress Cycle and Successfully Engage with Life.* Penguin Press.

> **Summary:** Self-Regulation theory framework emphasizing that before students can learn social skills, their stress response systems must be regulated. Identifies five domains of stressors: biological, emotional, cognitive, social, prosocial.
> **FORGE Application:** The Calm Points mechanic directly operationalizes Self-Reg principles. The Lantern Check-In at session start addresses the biological/emotional domains before cognitive work begins.

## Part 4: Trauma, ACEs, and Narrative

**Felitti, V. J., Anda, R. F., Nordenberg, D., Williamson, D. F., Spitz, A. M., Edwards, V., & Marks, J. S. (1998).** Relationship of childhood abuse and household dysfunction to many of the leading causes of death in adults. *American Journal of Preventive Medicine, 14*(4), 245–258.

> **Summary:** The foundational ACEs study. Established that adverse childhood experiences are common, cumulative, and produce lasting physiological and psychological harm. 64% of participants reported at least one ACE.
> **FORGE Application:** Establishes the prevalence of trauma in the student populations FORGE serves. Justifies the trauma-informed design principles that permeate every FORGE document.

---

**Malchiodi, C. A. (2011).** *Trauma and Expressive Arts Therapy: Brain, Body, and Imagination in the Healing Process.* Guilford Press.

> **Summary:** Reviews neuroscience and clinical evidence for expressive arts (including narrative and play) as trauma interventions. Argues that trauma is stored somatically and subcortically, and that narrative approaches that engage imagination and sensory experience are uniquely effective.
> **FORGE Application:** The imaginative, narrative nature of FORGE is not incidental — it directly engages the brain systems most impacted by trauma. Supports the use of FORGE with students with trauma histories.

---

**Gersie, A. (1997).** *Reflections on Therapeutic Storymaking: The Use of Stories in Groups.* Jessica Kingsley Publishers.

> **Summary:** Classic text on narrative approaches in group therapy. Argues that myth, archetype, and collective story provide a "container" for individual experiences too painful to address directly. Group story creation builds cohesion and reduces isolation.
> **FORGE Application:** The shared world-building elements of FORGE (students help design Aurenvast) reflect this principle. The adventure serves as a collective container for individual experiences.

---

**SAMHSA. (2014).** *SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach.* HHS Publication No. (SMA) 14-4884. Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration.

> **Summary:** Defines trauma-informed care and its six principles: Safety, Trustworthiness & Transparency, Peer Support, Collaboration & Mutuality, Empowerment/Voice/Choice, Cultural/Historical/Gender Issues.
> **FORGE Application:** Each SAMHSA principle is explicitly embedded in FORGE's design. This document is the blueprint for FORGE's trauma-informed architecture. See `docs/03_THERAPEUTIC_FRAMEWORK.md` for full alignment mapping.

## Part 5: School-Based Mental Health

**Hoagwood, K. E., Olin, S. S., Kerker, B. D., Kratochwill, T. R., Crowe, M., & Saka, N. (2007).** Empirically based school interventions targeted at academic and mental health functioning. *Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders, 15*(2), 66–92.

> **Summary:** Review of evidence-based school mental health interventions. Found that group-based interventions delivered by school mental health professionals in school settings are effective and cost-efficient, with particular strength in social skills and anxiety reduction.
> **FORGE Application:** Validates the delivery model — group intervention, in school, delivered by trained school mental health professional.

---

**Weist, M. D., Lever, N. A., Bradshaw, C. P., & Owens, J. S. (Eds.). (2014).** *Handbook of School Mental Health.* Springer.

> **Summary:** Comprehensive review of school mental health research. Emphasizes MTSS framework, tiered support, and the importance of universal screening to identify students for Tier 2 and 3 services.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE's MTSS alignment (Tier 2 primary; Tier 3 adaptable) reflects best-practice school mental health organization.

---

**Kern, L., & Manz, P. (2004).** A look at current validity issues of school-wide behavior support. *Behavioral Disorders, 30*(1), 47–59.

> **Summary:** Examines PBIS as a framework for school-wide behavioral support. Highlights the need for Tier 2 interventions that address social skills deficits, not just behavioral consequences.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE serves as a Tier 2 social skills intervention that complements school-wide PBIS. Students referred through PBIS data (repeated behavioral patterns suggesting skill deficits) are ideal FORGE candidates.

## Part 6: Game-Based Learning

**Mayer, R. E. (2019).** Computer games in education. *Annual Review of Psychology, 70*, 531–549. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-psych-010418-102744

> **Summary:** Reviews evidence that well-designed games promote learning through engagement, active processing, and meaningful feedback loops. Notes that the social context of games (playing with others) adds additional learning benefits beyond solo play.
> **FORGE Application:** Supports the engagement rationale for FORGE — students who resist traditional social skills groups often willingly participate in game-based formats.

---

**Connolly, T. M., Boyle, E. A., MacArthur, E., Hainey, T., & Boyle, J. M. (2012).** A systematic literature review of empirical evidence on computer games and serious games. *Computers & Education, 59*(2), 661–686.

> **Summary:** Systematic review of 129 papers on educational games. Found consistent evidence for motivational benefits and moderate evidence for learning outcomes. Social games (multiplayer) showed additional benefits for collaboration and communication skills.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE is fundamentally a social game — the multiplayer, collaborative design is not just fun but therapeutically active.

---

**Vygotsky, L. S. (1967).** Play and its role in the mental development of the child. *Soviet Psychology, 5*(3), 6–18. (Original work published 1933)

> **Summary:** Classic developmental theory establishing play as the "zone of proximal development" — children (and adolescents) use play to operate at their highest level of capability. In play, children exceed their typical functioning.
> **FORGE Application:** The developmental case for using play-based approaches with adolescents. Middle school students remain in the zone where play accelerates development, despite cultural pressure to abandon it.

---

## Part 7: Cultural Responsiveness

**Sue, D. W., & Sue, D. (2012).** *Counseling the Culturally Diverse: Theory and Practice* (6th ed.). Wiley.

> **Summary:** Foundational text on multicultural counseling. Argues that culturally unresponsive interventions produce harm even when well-intentioned. Identifies specific adaptations needed for diverse populations.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE archetypes are deliberately culture-neutral (not rooted in Western fantasy tropes exclusively). Facilitators are guided to invite students to define their world's cultural norms. See `docs/02_FACILITATOR_GUIDE.md`.

---

**Yohanna, D. (2013).** Deinstitutionalization of people with mental illness: Causes and consequences. *Virtual Mentor, 15*(10), 886–891.

> **Summary:** Historical context for why community-based, school-embedded mental health services are now carrying the load of population mental health. Schools have become de facto mental health systems.
> **FORGE Application:** Contextualizes the urgency of school-based programs like FORGE. Social workers are often the last line of mental health support for students.

## Part 8: Theoretical Frameworks Underlying FORGE Design

**Bandura, A. (1997).** *Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control.* W. H. Freeman.

> **Summary:** Self-efficacy — belief in one's capacity to succeed — predicts achievement, coping, and resilience. The four sources of self-efficacy: mastery experiences, vicarious experiences, social persuasion, physiological states.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE generates mastery experiences (succeeding at challenges), vicarious experiences (watching other characters succeed), and social persuasion (group encouragement). Character growth/leveling is a visible self-efficacy scaffold.

---

**Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000).** Self-determination theory and the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and well-being. *American Psychologist, 55*(1), 68–78. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.55.1.68

> **Summary:** Self-Determination Theory identifies three universal psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Meeting these needs produces intrinsic motivation, well-being, and growth.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE systematically meets all three needs. Students choose their characters (autonomy), experience mastery through the game (competence), and build genuine peer relationships (relatedness).

---

**White, M., & Epston, D. (1990).** *Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends.* Norton.

> **Summary:** Foundational narrative therapy text. Argues that problems are not inherent to people — they are stories that have been adopted. Therapy involves "re-authoring" — helping people write new, more empowering stories about themselves.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE players are literally re-authoring themselves through their characters. The debrief explicitly connects character stories to personal narratives. The FORGE slogan could be: "You are the author of your character's story. You can be the author of your own."

---

**Bronfenbrenner, U. (1979).** *The Ecology of Human Development.* Harvard University Press.

> **Summary:** Ecological systems theory: human development occurs across nested systems (microsystem → mesosystem → exosystem → macrosystem). School is a critical microsystem; the quality of peer relationships within school is a key developmental driver.
> **FORGE Application:** FORGE operates within the school microsystem and directly targets the peer relationship environment. It also creates a bridge between school and family (parent communication tools).

## A Note on Evidence Level

FORGE RPG is evidence-*informed*, not (yet) evidence-*based* by the strictest APA criteria. The distinction:

- **Evidence-based:** Multiple randomized controlled trials specifically testing THIS intervention
- **Evidence-informed:** Strong theoretical basis + research on component mechanisms + pilot/quasi-experimental data

FORGE occupies a position similar to many established school programs (Restorative Practices, Social Thinking, Check-In/Check-Out) — supported by a strong and growing evidence base, used responsibly by trained professionals.

We invite researchers to conduct pilot studies using FORGE RPG. See the Theory of Change document and contact the project maintainers for collaboration.
