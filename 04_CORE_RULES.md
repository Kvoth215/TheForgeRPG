# ⚔️ FORGE RPG Core Rules
## The Complete Game System for School-Based Play
**Facilitator & Student Edition**

---

## Design Philosophy

FORGE RPG rules are intentionally **lighter than Dungeons & Dragons**. We removed complexity that creates barriers and kept only what serves the therapeutic purpose. If a rule isn't helping students grow, it isn't in this book.

The goal of every mechanic is to create moments where students **practice real skills in a low-stakes imaginary space.**

---

## The HEART Attributes

Every character has five core attributes, called **HEART**. Each maps directly to a CASEL SEL competency.

| Attribute | What It Represents | In the Game | SEL Competency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **H**ead | Thinking, planning, memory | Solving puzzles, recalling information | Responsible Decision-Making |
| **E**mpathy | Understanding others | Reading NPC emotions, convincing people | Social Awareness |
| **A**gility | Quick action, physical skill | Moving, reacting, dodging | Self-Management |
| **R**esolve | Emotional strength, willpower | Resisting fear, staying calm under pressure | Self-Management |
| **T**eamwork | Collaboration, support | Helping others, communicating | Relationship Skills |

### Starting Scores

Each character archetype begins with a set of HEART scores reflecting their strengths. Scores range from **1 (developing) to 5 (exceptional)**.

A score of **3 is average** — most real people land around 3 in most areas.

**Important facilitator note:** The scores represent the *character*, not the student. A student with significant anxiety can play a high-Resolve character without contradiction — in fact, this is therapeutically useful.

## The Core Resolution Mechanic

When a character attempts something with uncertain outcome:

### Step 1: Name What You're Trying to Do
The student says what their character is attempting, clearly.
> *"Mira tries to convince the guard to let us through by explaining we're there to help."*

### Step 2: Identify the HEART Attribute
The facilitator (or the group) identifies which attribute applies.
> *Convincing someone → Empathy*

### Step 3: Roll a d20
Roll one 20-sided die.

### Step 4: Add Your Attribute Score
Add the relevant HEART attribute to the die roll.
> *Die = 12, Empathy = 3 → Total = 15*

### Step 5: Compare to Difficulty

| Difficulty | Target Number | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Easy | 8 | Asking for directions |
| Medium | 12 | Persuading a skeptical stranger |
| Hard | 16 | Convincing a frightened NPC to trust you |
| Very Hard | 20 | Changing someone's deep-held belief |

### Step 6: Describe the Outcome

| Result | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Meets or exceeds** the target | Success — the character achieves their goal |
| **Within 3 below** the target | Partial success — achieved with a cost or complication |
| **More than 3 below** | Failure — but failure always teaches something |

**Critical FORGE Rule:** Failure is never "you lose" — it is always "what can you learn from this?" The facilitator frames all failures as learning opportunities, not defeats.

## Advantage and Disadvantage

Some situations make things easier or harder than normal.

**Advantage:** Roll two d20s. Use the **higher** number.

*Triggered by:* having help from an ally, using the right tool, taking time to prepare, or a previous success that built confidence.

**Disadvantage:** Roll two d20s. Use the **lower** number.

*Triggered by:* being exhausted or stressed, acting against the character's nature, acting too quickly without thinking, or a recent failure that shook confidence.

**Important:** Facilitators use Advantage and Disadvantage intentionally to scaffold success — struggling students should receive Advantage more often.

## The Help Action — Mechanically Rewarding Collaboration

Any student (whose character is present) can declare they are **helping** another character's action.

**How it works:**
1. The helping student describes how their character helps
2. If the description makes sense, the acting character gains **Advantage**
3. Helping does not require a roll

**Therapeutic purpose:** This mechanic makes collaboration a **direct mechanical benefit**, not just a social nicety. Students who help others are visibly, immediately rewarded.

**Facilitator move:** When a student who struggles to initiate connection spontaneously offers help, acknowledge it — both in-game ("That assistance was exactly what Mira needed!") and, briefly, out-of-game ("Nice teamwork.").

## Calm Points — The Emotion Regulation Mechanic

This is FORGE's most therapeutically significant mechanic.

**Every character has a Calm Points track** — visualized as a five-cell battery bar on the character sheet. All characters start each session with **3 Calm Points** (out of 5).

### Losing Calm Points

Characters lose Calm Points when they experience:
- A significant failure (lose 1)
- A frightening or threatening situation (lose 1)
- A conflict with another character (lose 1)
- Being separated from the group (lose 1)
- Being surprised or deceived (lose 1)

### Gaining Calm Points

Characters regain Calm Points when they:
- Help another character successfully (gain 1)
- Reach a safe place (gain 1)
- Complete a meaningful task (gain 1)
- Connect meaningfully with an NPC (gain 1)
- Use a coping strategy (described below) (gain 1)

### What Calm Points Do

| Calm Points | Character State | Mechanical Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Calm and focused | All rolls at normal |
| 4 | Slightly stressed | All rolls at normal |
| 3 | Worried | All rolls at normal |
| 2 | Stressed | Disadvantage on Empathy and Resolve rolls |
| 1 | Overwhelmed | Disadvantage on all rolls |
| 0 | Crisis | Character cannot act effectively; facilitator intervention required |

**When a character reaches 0 Calm Points:**

This is a **story moment**, not a failure state. The facilitator pauses the game:

> *"Your character is having a really hard time right now. What does [character name] need most in this moment?"*

This opens a conversation about:
- What strategies the character might use
- What support they need from their companions
- What the student needs (optionally — let the student lead this)

### Coping Strategies in the Game

At character creation, each student chooses **two coping strategies** for their character. These are drawn from real evidence-based strategies, described in character-appropriate language:

| Real Strategy | In-Game Name |
|---|---|
| Deep breathing | "Breath of the Wind" |
| Grounding (5-4-3-2-1) | "Reading the Room" |
| Self-talk / positive reframe | "The Inner Voice" |
| Seeking social support | "Call to Companions" |
| Physical movement | "Warrior's Walk" |
| Mindful pause | "The Still Moment" |
| Cognitive reappraisal | "Seeing It Different" |

When a character uses a coping strategy in-game (described by the student), they regain 1 Calm Point **and** the facilitator has a natural opportunity to connect the in-game strategy to real life during the debrief.

## Social Encounters — The Heart of FORGE

Most FORGE encounters involve talking with NPCs (Non-Player Characters) or making group decisions. These are called **Social Encounters**.

### The Social Encounter Structure

**1. Set the Scene**
The facilitator describes the NPC and situation vividly, emphasizing observable emotional cues.
> *"The old woman at the gate looks tired. Her hands are shaking slightly. She's scanning the crowd like she's looking for someone."*

**2. Read the Room**
Before acting, students can ask:
- "What does this person seem to be feeling?"
- "What might they need?"
- "Is there anything we should know before approaching?"

Reading the Room is an Empathy check (Target 10). Success gives extra information.

**3. Engage**
Students describe what their characters say and do. The facilitator plays the NPC responsively — NPCs have real reactions, change their minds, get confused, feel grateful.

**4. Navigate**
Conversations may require multiple rolls. NPCs may have:
- **A Need** — something they want
- **A Fear** — something they're worried about
- **A Secret** — something they haven't shared yet

Effective social encounters address the NPC's Need and Fear.

**5. Debrief (brief)**
After each significant social encounter, the facilitator may ask:
- "What did you notice about how [NPC name] was feeling?"
- "What worked? What would you do differently?"
- "Has anyone ever been in a situation like [NPC's situation]?"

## Physical Encounters — Combat (Simplified)

FORGE uses highly simplified combat, designed to minimize the appeal of violence as the primary problem-solving tool.

**FORGE Design Choice:** Social encounters are mechanically *more rewarding* than physical encounters. Violence is a last resort with real narrative costs — it's not glamorized.

### Combat Rules

**Initiative:** Each character rolls a d20. Highest goes first.

**Attack:** Roll d20 + Agility vs. Target Defense (varies by enemy, usually 10-14)

**Damage:** Success = enemy weakened. After 3 successes, enemy is defeated.

**Taking Hits:** Instead of Hit Points, characters lose **Calm Points** when hit (1 per significant hit). This connects physical challenges to emotional impact.

**Talking Instead:** Players can always choose to **negotiate rather than fight**. If they successfully use Social Encounter rules during combat, the combat ends without anyone being defeated.

**Facilitator Note:** Actively make the social resolution option appealing. NPCs that players "defeat" in conversation become **allies**. NPCs defeated in combat are simply gone.

## Leveling Up — Representing Growth

After completing each adventure module (8 sessions), characters level up.

**What leveling up means:**
- +1 point to one HEART attribute (student chooses)
- One new **strength** (a special capability reflecting character growth)
- A **Growth Story** — the student writes 1-2 sentences describing how their character grew

**The Growth Story reflection prompt:**

> *"Your character started this adventure as one kind of person and ended it as someone slightly different. What changed? What did they learn? What are they proud of?"*

**Therapeutic purpose:** Leveling up creates a structured narrative of growth. Over multiple modules, students accumulate a portfolio of Growth Stories — a concrete record of their character's (and, metaphorically, their own) development.

## The FORGE Session Arc — Every Session, Every Time

Consistency is therapeutic. Every FORGE session follows the same 5-part arc. Students learn what to expect, which builds safety.

**Recommended timing for a 45-minute session:**

| Segment | Time | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **1. The Lantern Check-In** | 5 min | Emotional temperature; transition into the space |
| **2. The Recap** | 5 min | Memory, continuity, group cohesion |
| **3. The Adventure** | 25 min | Core therapeutic play |
| **4. The Debrief** | 8 min | Bridge from game to real life |
| **5. The Closing Ritual** | 2 min | Grounding, transition back to school day |

### 1. The Lantern Check-In

Each student (and the facilitator) holds up fingers showing how they feel today: 1 = really rough, 5 = great. No explanation required unless the student wants to share.

If any student shows 1 or 2: check in privately and consider whether play should proceed or a different kind of support is needed.

### 2. The Recap

Ask the group: "Last time, your heroes..." Let students reconstruct the story from memory. Correct gently if needed. This builds narrative memory and signals that their contributions matter.

### 3. The Adventure

Follow the encounter guide for the current module session. See individual module files.

### 4. The Debrief

Use structured questions to bridge in-game experiences to real-world learning. Always begin with the character, then invite personal connection:

- "How did [character name] handle that situation?"
- "What was hard about that for your character?"
- "Has anyone ever faced something like that in real life?" (Never required — student's choice)
- "What's one thing your character did today that you're proud of?"

### 5. The Closing Ritual

The same ritual every session. Options:
- **The Shield Circle:** Each student names one thing their character will protect or carry forward
- **The Victory Shout:** The group invents a group cheer (changes each module)
- **The Compass:** Each student points in a direction they want to head next (literal or metaphorical)

## Facilitator Fiat — When to Bend the Rules

You are always in charge of the story. Rules serve therapy, not the other way around.

**When to use Facilitator Fiat:**
- A student is close to a breakthrough and a roll shouldn't derail it → just let them succeed
- A student needs a challenge to practice resilience → add a complication regardless of the roll
- A student is dysregulating → pause the game entirely, attend to the student
- The story is moving away from the therapeutic target → redirect through NPC behavior or plot twist
- A moment of genuine connection between students happens → let the game pause to honor it

**The Golden Rule:** The story exists to serve the students. The rules exist to serve the story.

---

## A Note on the Magic

Something real happens in these sessions. Students who can barely maintain eye contact in the hallway will negotiate complex social situations with confidence when they're playing a Guardian named Thornwood or a Seeker named Lyra.

That's not magic — it's therapeutic distance, self-efficacy, and the power of play.

But it feels like magic.

Trust the process.
