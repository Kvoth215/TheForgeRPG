# 🛡️ FORGE RPG Safety Tools
## Emotional & Psychological Safety Protocols
**Required Reading for All Facilitators — Complete Before Session Zero**

---

> *"Safety is not just the absence of danger. It is the active presence of welcome."*

---

## Why Safety Tools Are Non-Negotiable

FORGE RPG creates a space where students practice vulnerability. That's the therapeutic mechanism — and it's also the source of real risk.

Students experiencing trauma, anxiety, or social pain may have those experiences **activated by the game** — either by content in the story, or by the interpersonal dynamics of the group. This is not a failure; it is the game doing what games do: bringing real life into the imaginary space.

Safety tools are the structure that makes this activation **therapeutic rather than retraumatizing.**

Every tool in this document should be:
1. **Introduced in Session Zero** — before any play begins
2. **Available every session** — physically or verbally accessible
3. **Practiced** — not just explained; students should use them at least once before they need them
4. **Destigmatized** — using a safety tool is a sign of self-awareness and courage, not weakness

---

## The Safety Tool Suite

### 1. 🃏 The X-Card

**What it is:** A physical card (index card with a large X) placed in the center of the table. Any student or facilitator can tap it at any time to signal that the current content needs to stop or change — no explanation required.

**How to introduce it (Session Zero script):**

> *"See this card? This is called the X-Card. It belongs to all of us. If anything we're doing in the game ever makes you uncomfortable — for any reason, and you never have to explain why — you can tap this card. I'll stop what I'm happening, check in with the group, and we'll move in a different direction. I might use it too. There's no wrong reason to use the X-Card."*

**How to respond when someone uses it:**

1. Stop immediately — no finishing the sentence
2. Say: "Thank you. We're pausing."
3. Do NOT ask why (unless the student volunteers)
4. Briefly: "Let's take a breath. [After pause:] Ready to continue in a different direction?"
5. Redirect the story away from whatever triggered the use
6. Continue when all students signal readiness

**After the session:** Check in privately with the student who used the X-Card. You do not need to process the trigger — simply ensure the student is okay and knows using the card was the right thing to do.

**Origin:** Developed by John Stavropoulos for convention gaming (2012); adapted here for school therapeutic contexts.

---

### 2. 🚪 The Open Door

**What it is:** A standing invitation for any student to leave the session space at any time without explaining why. Students can say "Open Door" or simply stand up and go.

**How to introduce it:**

> *"There may be a moment when you need to step outside — to get a drink of water, to take a breath, whatever you need. You can always say 'Open Door' and step out. No questions, no permission needed. I'll check on you in a few minutes if you haven't come back. The story will wait for you."*

**Important:** Know where students should go when they leave (counselor's office, hallway, etc.) and who is responsible for them. Have a plan before Session Zero.

**When a student uses it:**
- Let them leave without comment
- Continue briefly with the group, then step out yourself within 2 minutes
- Check in simply: "How are you doing? Do you need anything?"
- Students should never be required to return — the session continues without them if needed

---

### 3. 📏 Lines and Veils (Pre-Game Content Agreements)

**What it is:** A pre-game conversation about what content the group wants to avoid entirely (Lines) and what can be referenced but not explored in detail (Veils).

**In a school context, this is adapted:** Rather than a full Lines/Veils conversation (which can itself be activating for trauma-exposed students), facilitators pre-set content parameters and invite additions:

**Pre-set Lines (never in FORGE RPG):**
- Sexual content of any kind
- Graphic descriptions of violence or injury
- Content that mirrors real school violence scenarios
- Bullying scenes where students play the bully role

**Pre-set Veils (mentioned but not detailed):**
- Character death (referred to as "departure" or "sacrifice" — never graphic)
- Serious illness
- Family conflict

**Facilitator introduction:**

> *"In our story, certain things won't happen. There won't be anything inappropriate, and I'll skip over anything really scary or graphic. If there's anything you already know you don't want in our story, you can tell me privately — before or after any session. I'll make sure we don't go there."*

---

### 4. ✋ The PAUSE Signal

**What it is:** A non-verbal check-in system. Any student can hold up a flat hand (like a stop sign) to signal they need a moment without stopping the whole group.

The facilitator responds by making eye contact, nodding, and at the next natural pause in play, quietly checking in: "Need anything?"

**This is different from the X-Card** (which stops everything) — the PAUSE Signal is a private signal that something is off and a check-in is welcome.

**Introduce it:**

> *"If you ever need a moment but don't want to stop the whole game, just hold up your hand like this [demonstrate]. I'll see it and check in with you quietly."*

---

### 5. 🏮 The Lantern Check-In (Start of Every Session)

**What it is:** A brief emotional temperature check at the start of every session. Structured, non-invasive, takes less than 2 minutes.

**How it works:**
Each student (and facilitator) holds up fingers: **1** = having a really rough day, **5** = feeling great. No one has to explain their number.

**What to do with the information:**

| Score | Facilitator Response |
|---|---|
| 5 | Great — proceed normally |
| 4 | Normal — proceed |
| 3 | Average — proceed; keep an eye on this student |
| 2 | Struggling — check in briefly before play; consider a gentler encounter today |
| 1 | Hard day — private check-in before play; consider whether to play at all |

If most of the group shows 1-2: Consider doing a group check-in activity instead of the planned adventure. A low-energy connection activity may serve students better than a complex encounter.

**Therapeutic purpose:** The Lantern Check-In ensures the facilitator knows where each student is emotionally before the session begins, allowing real-time adaptation. It also models emotional self-awareness and normalizes checking in with oneself.

---

### 6. 🔚 The STOP Word

**What it is:** A single agreed-upon word that any student can say to immediately freeze the game. Stronger than the X-Card for students who are overwhelmed.

**Default STOP word in FORGE:** *"SHIELD"*

(You can collaboratively choose with the group in Session Zero — the word should be unusual enough that it won't occur naturally in play.)

**When STOP is called:**
1. All play stops immediately
2. Facilitator says: "We're stopping. Everyone take a breath."
3. 30-second pause (literal silence is okay)
4. Facilitator checks in with the student who called STOP
5. Decision: take a break, adjust the story, end the session early, or continue after a short rest

**STOP is not failure.** Introduce it this way:

> *"This word — 'SHIELD' — belongs to all of us. It's like an emergency brake. If anything ever feels like too much, say 'SHIELD' and we stop everything, no questions asked. I might use it too. Using 'SHIELD' is one of the bravest things you can do."*

---

### 7. 🌙 The Debrief Protocol (End of Every Session)

The session debrief is itself a safety tool — it provides a structured transition out of the imaginary space and back into reality.

**The 3-part debrief:**

**Part 1: The Story (2 minutes)**
What happened to the characters? Where did we leave them? (Past tense, in-character)

**Part 2: The Reflection (4 minutes)**
Structured questions moving from character to real life:
- "What was the hardest moment for your character today?"
- "What's something your character did that surprised you?"
- "If your character could give you one piece of advice, what would it be?"

**Part 3: The Landing (2 minutes)**
A brief grounding activity before students return to the school day:
- "Name one thing in this room that is definitely real and definitely okay."
- "Take a breath. You're [your name], and you're at school, and you're okay."
- Brief physical grounding: feet flat on floor, hands on table.

**Why this matters:** Without proper debrief and closing, students may remain partially in the "game state" — a mild dissociative posture that is not appropriate for a math class. The closing ritual and landing ground students back in their daily context.

---

### 8. 🔄 Re-Entry Protocol

**For students returning after missing a session:**

Simply say: "Welcome back. Last time, your group [brief recap]. Your character was [location/situation]. Ready to join back in?"

No processing of the absence required unless the student initiates.

**For the group when a student has been absent:**

Brief in-game explanation: "After their journey through the Quiet Forest, [character name] was separated from the group but has now returned."

Students should never feel that their absence created problems for the group.

## Recognizing When Play Should Stop

Some warning signs that indicate the session should be paused or ended:

**Pause play and check in when:**
- A student's emotional state is escalating (rising voice, agitation, withdrawal)
- Play content has unexpectedly mirrored a real student concern
- The group dynamic is becoming genuinely hostile (not character conflict — real conflict)
- A student appears dissociated or "far away"
- A student begins to cry (pause immediately; don't draw group attention; check in quietly)

**End the session early when:**
- A student has disclosed something requiring mandated reporting
- A student is in genuine emotional crisis
- The group has collectively dysregulated and re-regulation is not occurring
- A student requests to stop

**After ending early:**
Complete the paperwork. Contact parents if appropriate. Debrief with yourself (or a colleague) about what happened.

---

## A Word About the Weight of This Work

You will sit with students who carry heavy things. The game will surface some of those things. That is as it should be.

You will also witness moments of genuine joy, connection, and growth that you cannot manufacture in a traditional social skills group or counseling session.

Both will happen. That's what makes this work.

Take care of yourself. Supervision, consultation, and peer support are not optional for facilitators working with vulnerable populations.
