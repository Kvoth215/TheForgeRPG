## Quick Summary
A tabletop role-playing game (TTRPG) is a structured cooperative storytelling game where:
- Each player creates and plays a fictional character
- One facilitator (the "Game Master") guides the story and plays supporting characters
- Players describe what their characters do; outcomes are determined by dice rolls
- The story emerges from the group's collective decisions

There are no winners or losers. Success is cooperative.

## Why This Is Different From Video Games
TTRPGs are entirely social and verbal — no screens required. The imaginative engagement happens between people, not between a person and a machine. This social-verbal medium is precisely what makes TTRPGs therapeutically valuable for students who need social practice.

## Common Misconceptions Addressed
**"Isn't D&D dangerous?"** No. The 1980s moral panic about D&D has been thoroughly debunked by research. See Research Foundation for citations.

**"Isn't this just pretend play — too childish for middle schoolers?"** Research (Vygotsky, 1967; Harmon, 2018) confirms that play remains developmentally active through adolescence. Middle schoolers are in fact an ideal TTRPG population.

**"Will students get too into it / lose touch with reality?"** Structured debriefing at the end of every session maintains clear boundaries between game and reality. This is a core FORGE protocol.
