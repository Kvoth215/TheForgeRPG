## The Core Mechanism: Therapeutic Distance

Therapeutic distance is the psychological space created by the "as-if" frame of play. When a student plays a character, they can:

- Experience and express emotions that would be too threatening to own directly
- Practice behaviors that their anxiety would prevent in real life
- Receive feedback through the narrative (not direct criticism)
- Explore identity options without permanent commitment

This is why FORGE works where direct social skills instruction often doesn't.

## The Five Therapeutic Mechanisms in FORGE

**1. Mastery Experiences** (Bandura, 1997)
Characters succeed at challenges → builds self-efficacy belief → transfers to real-world confidence.

**2. Perspective-Taking Practice** (Rosselet & Stauffer, 2013)
Playing characters who are not yourself + interacting with NPCs who have different viewpoints = sustained theory of mind practice.

**3. Cognitive Reappraisal** (Gross, 2002)
"What would your character do differently?" prompts reappraisal of the situation — a more adaptive regulation strategy than suppression.

**4. Social Skill Rehearsal with Authentic Feedback** (Gresham et al., 2001)
NPCs respond realistically to character behavior. Ineffective social approaches produce realistic consequences — not punishment, but natural outcomes.

**5. Group Cohesion and Belonging** (Ryan & Deci, 2000)
Shared story creates shared identity. Students who can't connect with peers in the hallway often connect as adventurers working toward a common goal.
