## The Story

The characters wake up in a world unlike their own — the realm of **Aurenvast**, a patchwork land of forests, mountains, and ancient stone roads. They don't know how they arrived or how to get home.

They soon discover that Aurenvast is facing a quiet crisis: the two neighboring peoples of the realm — the **Thornweld** (who live in the forest) and the **Stonekin** (who live in the mountains) — have not spoken to one another in a generation. A great stone bridge that once connected their lands has fallen into disrepair. Trade has stopped. Children have grown up knowing the others only as strangers, or worse, as threats.

The characters are outsiders — neutral ground — and the only ones who might help rebuild the bridge. Both the Thornweld and Stonekin leaders are willing to talk to them, but not to each other. Not yet.

To rebuild the bridge, the characters must:
1. Earn the trust of both peoples
2. Help both sides understand each other's perspective
3. Work together across differences to rebuild something that no one can build alone

## Therapeutic Rationale

**Why this story?**

The Bridge Between Worlds is a deliberate metaphor for the social experience of students referred to FORGE groups. These students often:
- Feel like outsiders who don't fit in existing social groups
- See peer relationships as puzzling, hostile, or unsafe
- Lack strategies for initiating connection
- Experience communication as a barrier rather than a bridge

The story externalizes these experiences — students practice social navigation with characters and NPCs, not directly with each other (initially). As the module progresses and characters build trust with NPCs, students typically begin to build trust with each other.

By Session 8, the bridge they rebuild in the game is also the connection they've begun building in real life.

**Key therapeutic mechanisms:**
- Therapeutic distance (characters practice what students need to learn)
- Perspective-taking (the two peoples see the same situation very differently)
- Collaborative problem-solving (no single character can succeed alone)
- Earned trust (NPCs respond authentically to character behavior over time)
- Generalization (debrief bridges connect in-game to real life)

## The World of Aurenvast

**The Thornweld** — Forest people. Value: *listening and patience.*
- They speak carefully and are suspicious of quick talkers
- They communicate through nature metaphors and stories
- Key NPC: **Elder Maren** — cautious, wise, a little sad; lost family to a misunderstanding decades ago
- Secondary NPC: **Pip** — a young Thornweld child who immediately befriends the characters; serves as a social model

**The Stonekin** — Mountain people. Value: *honesty and directness.*
- They value getting to the point; elaborate politeness reads as deception to them
- They communicate through demonstrations and physical actions
- Key NPC: **Foreman Brek** — gruff but fair; deeply loyal; secretly scared of being wrong
- Secondary NPC: **Kova** — a teenage Stonekin apprentice who is curious about outsiders

**The Bridge** — once called the *Wanderer's Crossing*; built by a legendary figure named Liria who was, the stories say, "from both worlds and neither." The bridge's disrepair is both practical and symbolic.

**Tone:** Hopeful with moments of difficulty. Gentle challenge, not danger. This is a story about misunderstanding and connection, not about evil.

## Session 1 — Detailed Encounter Guide

### Arrival in Aurenvast

**Therapeutic Target:** Tolerating uncertainty; group introductions

**Calm Points Starting Position:** All characters begin at 3/5

**Opening narration (read or adapt):**

> *"You open your eyes and you're not where you expected to be. The air smells like pine and rain. The ground is soft beneath you. Around you, in a small clearing surrounded by tall, dark trees, are the others — people you know... sort of. And in front of you is a dirt road leading toward a cluster of warm lights in the distance.*
>
> *You don't know how you got here. You don't know where 'here' is. And then one of you says something, and this whole strange adventure begins.*
>
> *What does your character do first?"*

**Facilitator Notes:**

This opening is intentionally uncertain. The experience of waking up in an unknown place without clear instructions mirrors the feeling of starting a new social situation — the characters (and students) must self-organize without a script.

Watch for:
- Who takes initiative (leadership vs. waiting)
- Who checks on others (empathy behavior)
- Who looks for information vs. who acts immediately (planning vs. impulsivity)
- Who gets stuck (high anxiety response to uncertainty)

**First encounter — introductions:**

Before the characters reach the village, they meet Pip, the young Thornweld child, who is hiding in a tree watching them.

Pip will come down if: characters speak kindly, move slowly, and demonstrate they're not a threat. (Empathy check DC 10, or simply through described behavior if the description is empathic.)

Pip speaks in simple questions: "Are you lost? I was lost once. It's scary." "Do you have names? Names are important."

**Debrief questions (Session 1):**
- "What was it like to wake up somewhere unfamiliar? What did your character feel?"
- "Pip came down from that tree because your characters acted a certain way. What did you do that made that happen?"
- "Has anyone ever been the 'new person' in a situation? What would have helped?"

## Session 4 — Detailed Encounter Guide

### The Misunderstanding

**Therapeutic Target:** Perspective-taking; understanding how two people can both be right

**Setup:** The Thornweld and Stonekin had a conflict years ago over a pile of stones near the old bridge. Each side is certain the other acted wrongly. The characters discover that *both sides were partially right and partially wrong* — it was a genuine misunderstanding, not betrayal.

**The twist:** The characters must figure this out by gathering information from both sides, noticing the contradictions, and finding the truth.

**Mechanically:** This is a multi-stage Empathy and Head challenge. Characters who speak with both groups (without revealing what the other said) eventually piece together what really happened.

**Therapeutic purpose:** This encounter teaches that:
- People can genuinely remember the same event differently
- Neither memory is necessarily a lie
- Conflict often comes from misunderstanding, not malice
- Repair is possible even after real hurt

**The reveal (Facilitator guidance):**

> *"You've talked to Elder Maren and Foreman Brek. And you've realized something: they both told you the truth. Elder Maren really did believe the Stonekin took the stones on purpose. Foreman Brek really did believe the Thornweld were threatening them. Both of them were scared. Both of them reacted to their fear. And no one ever talked about it — until now. Until you."*

**Debrief questions (Session 4):**
- "Have you ever been in a fight where you were both right? What happened?"
- "What would it take for Maren and Brek to actually talk to each other?"
- "Is there a misunderstanding you've had with someone that might look like this?"

## Session 8 — The Celebration & Growth Stories

**The Final Session** has three parts:

**Part 1: The Bridge Crossing (20 min)**
The rebuilt bridge is complete. Both peoples gather. The characters walk across first — together. Both Elder Maren and Foreman Brek follow. NPCs the characters befriended over the module gather to witness.

This moment should feel earned and emotional. Don't rush it.

**Part 2: The Farewell (10 min)**
Each NPC the characters befriended gives them a parting gift — not a magic item, but a word or a memory:
- Pip: *"I'll remember that being kind to strangers is worth it."*
- Elder Maren: *"You listened when we needed it most."*
- Foreman Brek: *"You didn't give up. That matters."*

**Part 3: Growth Stories (15 min)**
Each student writes (or dictates, or draws) their character's Growth Story — 2-3 sentences about how their character changed during this adventure.

Then: each student shares one sentence from their Growth Story with the group.

**Closing ritual:** The group creates a new phrase — one that captures what they built together — and says it as a group.

## Adaptations

**For students with significant anxiety (high avoidance):**
- Allow characters to observe before acting in Sessions 1-2
- Give Advantage on all Empathy rolls in Sessions 1-3 (scaffold early success)
- Offer the option to write instead of speak for character actions

**For students with ADHD or executive function challenges:**
- Keep a visual "quest tracker" on the table (what are we trying to do?)
- Use 3D tokens or minis to make the spatial environment concrete
- Provide more frequent Calm Points opportunities

**For students with trauma histories:**
- Increase use of the PAUSE signal check-ins
- Be prepared to redirect the misunderstanding narrative if it activates personal trauma
- The NPC gift-giving in Session 8 may be emotionally significant — allow processing time
